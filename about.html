Background

My interest in electronics started with understanding how transistors amplify signals and grew into a fascination with how billions of them, arranged just right, can process information efficiently. At NIT Surat, I've been working across domains — from RTL design and FPGA implementation to neuromorphic computing and RF circuit analysis.

What I find most interesting is the problem of doing more with less: fewer watts, fewer gate delays, smaller silicon area. That tradeoff space is where I spend most of my time thinking.

How I work

01

Start from constraints

Before writing a single line of HDL or code, I try to understand what the system actually needs — power envelope, latency budget, area targets. Most design decisions downstream become easier when you're clear about the constraints up front.

02

Architect at the right level of abstraction

Good hardware design starts at the block level: defining the datapath, control unit interactions, and timing before getting into RTL. For the EEG project, settling the SNN architecture before implementation saved significant iteration time.

03

Verify before you trust

Self-checking testbenches in Vivado, parametric sweeps in LTspice, LOPO cross-validation for the neural network — I don't consider something done until it's been stressed at boundary conditions and the numbers make sense.
