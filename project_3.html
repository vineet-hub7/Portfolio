Project 03 · RF / Analog

WaveShield RF

A 2G GSM signal jammer — swept-frequency RF interference generator targeting the 900 MHz and 1800 MHz cellular bands.

Abstract

Mobile communication depends on a clean RF link between the handset and the base station. WaveShield RF disrupts this link by generating wideband noise across the GSM-900 and GSM-1800 frequency bands. Using a voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO) driven by a sawtooth sweep generator, the system produces continuous swept interference that prevents mobile devices within range from establishing or maintaining a cellular connection. This project was built as an academic study of RF interference fundamentals and spectrum denial techniques.

Sweep oscillator

A sawtooth waveform generator drives the VCO tuning voltage, causing the output frequency to sweep continuously across the target band. The sweep rate is chosen to ensure that no GSM time slot can complete a full frame without encountering interference.

Power amplification

The VCO output is boosted through an RF power amplifier stage to achieve sufficient radiated power for local-area coverage. Band-pass filtering ensures energy stays within the target GSM bands, minimizing out-of-band emissions.

What I learned

This project was about understanding RF from the ground up — how oscillators generate carrier frequencies, how amplifiers shape radiated power, and how cellular protocols like GSM handle (or fail to handle) interference. Working with analog RF circuits taught me things that simulation alone cannot: the reality of impedance matching, parasitic capacitance, and the difference between a schematic that works in LTspice and a PCB that actually radiates. These lessons directly inform my understanding of mixed-signal and communication system design.
